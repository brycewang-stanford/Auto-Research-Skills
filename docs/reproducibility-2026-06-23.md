# Can You Reproduce an "AI Scientist"? A Maintainer's Reading

A critical, evidence-grounded note on the reproducibility of the autonomous-
research systems this hub vendors. The headline claim of the field — agents that
"go from idea to peer-reviewed paper" — is exciting, but reproducibility is a
separate question from capability. This note grounds that question first in data
we can measure directly from the vendored repos, then in the evaluation
substrate, then in the literature.

It is opinion, clearly labeled, not a benchmark result.

---

## 1. The first reproducibility tax is upstream hygiene — and we measured it

Before a single experiment runs, two boring properties decide whether a system
can be reproduced and reused at all: **does it have a license**, and **is it
still maintained against today's model APIs**. We now track both per repo in
[`../catalog/HEALTH.md`](../catalog/HEALTH.md) (generated by
`scripts/check-submodule-health.py`). The picture for the flagship
"full-autonomy" systems is sobering:

| System | ⭐ | License | Last push | Reproduction friction |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| `systems/ai-scientist` | 14.1k | NOASSERTION | 6mo | Legal reuse unclear; pinned to older model APIs |
| `systems/ai-scientist-v2` | 6.7k | NOASSERTION | 6mo | Same |
| `systems/ai-researcher` | 5.5k | **none** | 8mo | All-rights-reserved by default |
| `systems/auto-deep-research` | 1.6k | **none** | 8mo | All-rights-reserved by default |
| `systems/coscientist` | 206 | NOASSERTION | 10mo | Legal reuse unclear |
| `systems/sciagents` | 618 | Apache-2.0 | 1y1m | Stale vs. current model APIs |
| `systems/chemcrow` | 928 | MIT | 1y6m | Tool integrations likely bit-rotted |
| `systems/autosurvey` | 470 | **none** | 1y4m | License + staleness |
| `systems/popper` | 275 | **none** | 1y1m | License + staleness |

That is roughly a third of the `systems/` shelf carrying a **license gap**, a
**staleness flag**, or both. Two observations:

- **License gaps are a hard reproducibility blocker.** A repo with no license is
  all-rights-reserved by default — you cannot legally redistribute or build on
  it, regardless of how good the science is. `NOASSERTION` means GitHub could not
  detect a standard license (often a non-OSI or content-only license). Either
  way, a result you cannot legally rerun-and-extend is only half-reproducible.
- **Staleness compounds fast in this field.** These systems are tightly coupled
  to specific model endpoints and SDK versions. A system last touched 12–18
  months ago was written against models and APIs that have since changed or been
  retired. "It ran in the paper" and "it runs today" diverge quickly.

This is the part of the reproducibility story that needs no literature to
establish — it is measurable, and we measure it on every refresh.

## 2. Benchmarks measure capability, not reproducible discovery

The hub deliberately vendors the evaluation substrate alongside the systems —
most aggregators skip this:

- [`benchmarks/scienceagentbench`](../benchmarks/scienceagentbench) — data-driven
  scientific tasks.
- [`benchmarks/mlagentbench`](../benchmarks/mlagentbench) — end-to-end ML
  experimentation.
- [`benchmarks/discoverybench`](../benchmarks/discoverybench) — data-driven
  discovery.
- [`benchmarks/aviary`](../benchmarks/aviary) — a language-agent gym of
  scientific tasks.

These are necessary and valuable, but note what they do and do not certify. A
high score says an agent completed scored tasks under a harness; it does **not**
say the agent's broader claims reproduce, that its "novel" idea was actually
novel, or that the harness itself is reproducible (DiscoveryBench and
MLAgentBench themselves show staleness flags in HEALTH.md). Leaderboard progress
and reproducible science are correlated, not identical.

## 3. What the literature argues (attributed, not endorsed)

Surfaced via the 2026-06-23 landscape sweep (see
[`landscape-2026.md`](landscape-2026.md)); these are pointers, not claims we
have independently verified:

- *Why LLMs Aren't Scientists Yet: Lessons from Four Autonomous Research
  Attempts* (arXiv 2601.03315) — argues the systematic weak points are novelty
  judgment and experimental rigor, not raw code-writing ability.
- *A Survey of AI Scientists* (arXiv 2510.23045) — a map of the design space.
- *The AI Scientist-v2* (arXiv 2504.08066) — reports one fully-autonomous
  manuscript clearing a workshop review bar, the field's most-cited capability
  milestone — and a useful reminder that "passed review once" is a single
  data point, not a reproducibility result.

The consistent theme: the bottleneck has moved from *can it produce a paper-
shaped artifact* (largely yes) to *is the science sound, novel, and
reproducible* (often unclear).

## 4. A taxonomy of where reproduction breaks

Combining the measured data above with the literature, the failure modes stack:

1. **Harness reproducibility** — can you even rerun the system? Blocked by
   missing licenses, stale deps, pinned-but-retired model endpoints, and
   undisclosed prompts/seeds. This is the layer HEALTH.md surfaces.
2. **Result reproducibility** — rerun, do you get the same finding? Threatened by
   model nondeterminism, provider-side model updates, and unlogged tool versions.
3. **Scientific validity** — even if it reproduces, is the claim novel and
   correct? The layer the literature flags as weakest, and the one no benchmark
   score fully certifies.

A system can pass (1) and (2) and still fail (3) — and many flagship systems
currently stumble at (1) on license/staleness grounds before the science is even
in question.

## 5. What this hub does about it

Aggregation-with-provenance is the differentiated stance, and it maps onto the
taxonomy:

- **Layer 1:** `HEALTH.md` tracks license + staleness per repo, so a maintainer
  sees reproduction friction before recommending a system. License gaps are
  flagged in the README (e.g. POPPER's ⚠️) rather than hidden.
- **Cross-checks:** `scripts/scan-skill-safety.py` + `catalog/SAFETY.md` audit
  what an agent might execute; `catalog/QUALITY.md` tracks redundancy and
  per-skill license declaration.
- **Layer 2/3:** vendoring the benchmark suite keeps evaluation in view rather
  than taking capability claims at face value.

## 6. Recommendations

- **For users:** treat a vendored system's paper claims as a starting point;
  check its HEALTH row first. A NOASSERTION/stale flagship is a research artifact
  to study, not a turn-key pipeline to trust.
- **For the hub:** file upstream license issues on the no-license flagships
  (`ai-researcher`, `auto-deep-research`, `autosurvey`, `popper`); consider a
  periodic `make health-report` so staleness is visible as it accrues.
- **For the field:** the cheapest reproducibility win is unglamorous — ship a
  license, pin and log model/SDK versions, and publish prompts and seeds. Most
  of the systems here would move from "half-reproducible" to "reproducible" with
  no new research at all.
